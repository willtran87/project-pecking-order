# Pecking Order

## Game Concept Document

### High Concept

*Pecking Order* is a darkly comic workplace-management game set inside a corporate chicken coop. Hens peck at screens to produce work and lay eggs as deliverables. Roosters supervise, evaluate, and motivate them. At the end of each reporting period, the farmer collects the eggs, presents them as the result of his leadership, and receives all the credit.

The player must survive an organization whose cheerful language, colorful offices, and ridiculous rituals disguise a system built around extraction. They can pursue status, protect their coworkers, manipulate the metrics, organize resistance, or become part of the machine.

The satire should feel funny first, uncomfortable a moment later, and recognizable throughout.

---

## Player Role

The player begins as a newly hired hen assigned to a cramped workstation in a large corporate coop. Her immediate job is simple: peck the correct prompts on her screen, manage her energy and stress, and lay enough eggs to satisfy the daily quota.

As the game develops, the player gains influence over nearby workers and the wider office. Advancement creates new choices but also compromises. A successful hen may become a team lead, enter management, undermine the company from within, or reject the hierarchy entirely.

The central question is not simply, “Can I become successful?” It is, “What will success turn me into?”

---

## Design Pillars

### 1. Cute Presentation, Cruel Systems

The world is bright, tactile, and playful: pastel cubicles, tiny keyboards, motivational posters, neckties, coffee mugs, and carefully branded nests. Beneath that surface, every system measures, ranks, and extracts value from the birds.

### 2. Comedy Through Recognition

The jokes should emerge from recognizable workplace behavior rather than random absurdity. Corporate euphemisms, performative wellness, meaningless meetings, stolen credit, arbitrary metrics, and management fads all become literal through the farm setting.

### 3. Complicity Creates Better Choices

The player should rarely choose between obvious good and evil. Selfish behavior brings security and advancement. Solidarity costs time, feed, reputation, and personal safety. Management may genuinely improve the lives of a few chickens while preserving the system that harms everyone else.

### 4. Every Number Tells a Story

Productivity, morale, rank, fatigue, and egg quality are not merely resources. They reveal how the organization values its workers and encourage the player to question whether optimizing a metric actually improves anything.

---

## Core Gameplay Loop

1. **Receive the daily initiative.** Management announces a quota, policy, emergency, or fashionable new strategy.
2. **Work the shift.** Peck through tasks, manage interruptions, preserve energy, and produce eggs.
3. **Navigate the flock.** Help, compete with, inform on, or organize nearby chickens.
4. **Manipulate perception.** Decide what to report, conceal, exaggerate, or attribute to someone else.
5. **Attend the review.** Roosters judge performance using partially arbitrary metrics.
6. **Watch the farmer present.** The farmer collects the results, rewrites the story of the shift, and changes the organization for the next cycle.
7. **Spend influence.** Improve the player’s nest, help coworkers, buy status symbols, expose management, or build collective power.

Short-term play focuses on tactile pecking, interruptions, and resource management. Long-term play focuses on relationships, organizational politics, and the direction in which the player changes the coop.

---

## Primary Systems

### Pecking and Production

Pecking is the basic work action. On-screen tasks may involve rhythm, prioritization, pattern recognition, or rapid correction. Accurate pecks increase visible productivity, but constant pecking causes fatigue and beak strain.

Eggs function as completed deliverables. Their quality depends on more than speed:

- Energy and nutrition affect shell strength.
- Stress can produce cracked or unusual eggs.
- Collaboration can produce fewer but more valuable eggs.
- Rushed eggs satisfy quotas while creating problems later.
- Golden eggs are rare innovations that management aggressively claims.

The company continually encourages quantity, even when quality would produce better long-term results.

### Performance Metrics

The player is evaluated through deliberately questionable indicators:

- Peck velocity
- Screen-facing time
- Nest utilization
- Egg throughput
- Shell consistency
- Voluntary enthusiasm
- Synergy participation
- Leadership plumage

Some metrics contradict one another. Learning how the system measures performance becomes a game of its own. The player can perform real work, perform the appearance of work, or expose how meaningless the measurements are.

### Energy, Stress, and Beak Strain

Work drains energy and increases stress. Breaks, food, friendship, and small acts of rebellion restore the chicken, but they reduce measured productivity. If pushed too far, a chicken may burn out, stop laying, make mistakes, or be sent to the ominously named “Free Range Transition Program.”

The goal is to make overwork mechanically tempting. The player should understand why a chicken keeps pushing even when doing so is clearly harmful.

### Relationships and Solidarity

Coworkers remember how the player treats them. Sharing feed, covering a shift, correcting someone’s mistake, or taking blame builds trust. Competing for bonuses, stealing credit, and reporting dissent builds personal standing with management while weakening solidarity.

Trusted chickens can:

- Warn the player about new policies.
- Share shortcuts and hidden resources.
- Coordinate work slowdowns.
- Defend one another during reviews.
- Leak documents from upper management.
- Join a union or collective escape plan.

### Rank and Promotion

The pecking order is both social hierarchy and career ladder. Higher rank grants better food, larger nests, access to meetings, and control over other birds.

Promotion should always carry a cost. A hen promoted into management receives a tie, a desk, and authority, but gradually loses contact with production and may eventually lose the ability to lay eggs. The player gains the power to protect some chickens while becoming responsible for enforcing quotas on everyone else.

### Management and Credit

Roosters translate the farmer’s demands into daily pressure. Each rooster has a different management philosophy:

- The Believer sincerely loves every corporate initiative.
- The Survivor knows the system is absurd but enforces it to remain safe.
- The Climber steals credit and manufactures crises to look decisive.
- The Nice Boss offers kindness while refusing to challenge harmful policy.

At the end of each quarter, the farmer presents the flock’s eggs to unseen investors. The presentation changes reality: failures are blamed on workers, successes become leadership achievements, and inconvenient facts disappear behind polished charts.

## Satirical Themes and Their Representation

### Productivity Surveillance

Every desk is watched by the Henagement System, which measures pecks, pauses, bathroom visits, and “visible enthusiasm.” A large eye-shaped camera swivels toward idle workers. The player can fool it with a drinking bird toy, but doing so risks disciplinary action.

### Meaningless KPIs

Quarterly goals favor whatever management recently learned at a conference. The player may be ordered to maximize egg count, reduce egg count in favor of quality, then produce evidence that both policies were always consistent.

Metrics become increasingly detached from reality. A team can be praised while starving or punished after producing its best work.

### Credit Theft

Eggs physically move upward through the hierarchy. A hen lays one, a rooster stamps his name on it, and the farmer places it in a presentation basket. The visual journey makes invisible office appropriation literal.

The player can repeat this behavior after promotion, creating a strong moment of complicity: the same mechanic that once felt unfair now offers an easy path to success.

### Corporate Language as Horror

Management never uses direct language:

- Layoffs become “flock resizing.”
- Overwork becomes “discretionary peck commitment.”
- Surveillance becomes “engagement visibility.”
- Wage cuts become “feed flexibility.”
- Burnout becomes “temporary laying resistance.”
- Firings become “free-range opportunities.”

The euphemisms become more cheerful as conditions become worse.

### Performative Wellness

The company offers meditation nests, resilience seminars, compulsory gratitude exercises, and a monthly handful of premium feed. Participation is measured and creates more work. A wellness room may contain a poster reading, “You Cannot Pour From an Empty Crop,” beside a locked feed cabinet.

### Automation and Replaceability

A mechanical hen arrives promising perfect eggs without breaks. Initially it frightens the workers, but it is also overworked, poorly maintained, and blamed whenever leadership sets impossible expectations. The player may compete with it, sabotage it, befriend its operator, or discover that it mostly produces impressive-looking empty shells.

### Competition and Scarcity

Only the top-ranked chicken receives the weekly bonus, even when the entire flock performs well. Leaderboards turn minor differences into status. Feed, comfortable nests, healthcare, and even sunlight are allocated as performance rewards rather than basic needs.

### Unionization and Collective Action

Organizing begins through quiet conversations at the water cooler and coded scratches beneath desks. Collective action requires trust and exposes participants to retaliation. Management responds with listening sessions, selective promotions, friendly anti-union roosters, and a suspiciously generous catered feed party.

A union path should not be an instant victory. It creates new negotiations, disagreements, and responsibilities while proving that conditions can change when chickens act together.

### Identity and Corporate Culture

The company claims, “We are one flock,” while maintaining rigid distinctions between layers, roosters, contractors, free-range consultants, and executive exotic birds. Diversity initiatives may initially focus on feather colors in promotional material without changing who receives feed or authority.

The player can push these programs toward material change or exploit them as another route to status.

### Environmental Externalities

The coop’s relentless production pollutes the surrounding farm. Cracked eggs, discarded equipment, and waste accumulate just beyond the office windows. Management installs a green logo and announces a “net-zero shell” pledge while increasing quotas.

This expands the satire beyond individual bad managers: the organization exports costs that never appear on its dashboards.

## Tone and Presentation

The humor should be dry, visual, and systemic. Characters rarely acknowledge how strange the world is. A rooster earnestly discussing “nest-space optimization” is funnier than a character announcing that corporations are bad.

The darkness should escalate gradually:

- Early game: silly jargon, petty office politics, harmless absurdity.
- Midgame: exhaustion, favoritism, surveillance, and disappearing coworkers.
- Late game: automation, mass restructuring, ecological damage, and the player’s own complicity.

Avoid unbroken misery. Chickens should form friendships, tell jokes, decorate their cubicles, help each other, and find real dignity in their work. Those warm relationships give the darker moments weight.

Violence is best implied through farm language, silhouettes, sudden empty desks, and sanitized announcements. Restraint will keep the satire sharper than graphic cruelty.

## Recurring Characters

### The Player Hen

Customizable plumage, accessories, background, and initial motivation. Possible motivations include supporting a brood, seeking status, escaping a previous farm, or genuinely believing in the company mission.

### Cluckson, Direct Supervisor

An anxious middle-manager rooster caught between impossible farmer demands and the hens who must deliver them. He can become an antagonist, ally, cautionary example, or victim of the same system he enforces.

### Mabel, Veteran Layer

Knows every shortcut and remembers when the coop was smaller. She is cynical but protective. Management labels her “change resistant.”

### Pip, New Hire

Enthusiastic, trusting, and desperate to impress. The player’s treatment of Pip reflects what kind of worker—or manager—they are becoming.

### The Mechanical Hen

A polished automation system called E.G.G.-0. It appears threatening but slowly reveals signs of instability and exploitation. Its eggs may be flawless, hollow, or quietly copied from the hens’ previous work.

### The Farmer

Warm, charismatic, and almost never present. He speaks in broad visions, remembers few names, and sincerely considers himself the flock’s greatest protector. He receives applause whenever he enters and credit whenever he leaves.

## Example Events

### Mandatory Fun Friday

Workers must attend a morale event after their normal shift. Refusing harms their enthusiasm score. Participating restores some relationships but increases fatigue. The event’s eggs are added to the farmer’s community-engagement presentation.

### The Golden Egg

The player produces a breakthrough. They can give it to their supervisor, present it directly and violate protocol, hide it until they have leverage, or share credit with the team. Each choice affects promotion, trust, and ownership of future work.

### Flock Restructuring

Management orders the player to nominate a low performer. The dashboard makes one coworker appear expendable, but hidden information reveals why each bird has struggled. Refusal may place the player on the list.

### Feed Budget Optimization

The rooster asks the team to vote on whose feed allowance should be reduced. The vote creates the appearance of fairness while ensuring the cut happens. The player can manipulate the result, reject the premise, or uncover unused executive feed.

### Listening Tour

The farmer visits to hear honest feedback. Chickens may speak only through preapproved questions. The player can deliver a safe compliment, smuggle in a real concern, stage a protest, or use the visit for personal visibility.

### Bring Your Chick to Work Day

Young chicks visit the coop and imitate the adults. Familiar systems suddenly look more disturbing when explained to children. The event can build hope, deepen cynicism, or inspire organizing.

## Progression Structure

The game can be divided into four reporting quarters:

### Quarter 1: Welcome to the Flock

Learn pecking, egg production, basic relationships, and performance metrics. The office feels ridiculous but mostly benign.

### Quarter 2: Raise the Bar

Quotas rise. Rankings, wellness programs, and internal competition appear. The player chooses whether to prioritize personal advancement or coworkers.

### Quarter 3: Transformation Initiative

Automation arrives, restructuring begins, and the player gains enough influence to enter management or support organized resistance.

### Quarter 4: Record Harvest

The farmer needs spectacular results for a major presentation. Every system reaches its breaking point. The player decides what will be harvested: eggs, careers, solidarity, evidence, or the company itself.

---

## Possible Endings

### Farmer’s Favorite

The player rises through management and receives a portrait in the executive barn. The final scene reveals a new flock working beneath them under even higher quotas.

### Benevolent Rooster

The player protects their own team and creates a comfortable department, but the wider system remains intact. Their success depends on continuing to produce results for the farmer.

### Collective Bargaining

The flock wins meaningful protections and a share of the harvest. The victory is imperfect, administrative, and fragile—but real.

### Free Range

The player helps a group escape. Life outside is uncertain and materially harder, but their labor and eggs finally belong to them.

### Hostile Hen-Over

The chickens obtain evidence, seize the presentation, and reveal who created the value. The farmer is removed, only for the flock to confront the difficult task of running the coop differently.

### Perfect Optimization

The player automates everything and becomes the final remaining employee. The dashboard displays record productivity while the silent office fills with immaculate, empty eggs.

## Visual and Audio Direction

### Visual Style

- Cheerful storybook or clean corporate-cartoon aesthetic.
- Rounded shapes, warm feathers, pastel office furniture, and overly polished branding.
- Gradual environmental degradation as quotas rise: worn beaks, dimmer lights, crowded nests, more cameras, and fewer windows.
- Presentations use slick charts that visibly contradict the conditions behind the speaker.
- Hierarchy is communicated vertically: hens work low to the ground, roosters occupy raised platforms, and the farmer appears above everyone on enormous screens.

### Audio Style

- Light farm sounds blended with office ambience: clucks, keyboards, printers, fluorescent hum, and distant machinery.
- Pecking creates satisfying rhythmic percussion.
- Corporate jingles become faster and subtly more dissonant over time.
- Announcements use a calm, friendly voice regardless of their content.
- Moments of solidarity reduce the mechanical noise and allow natural farm sounds to return.

---

## Writing Principles

- Let the mechanics carry the argument; avoid speeches explaining the satire.
- Give managers understandable motives without excusing their behavior.
- Make workers flawed, funny, competitive, generous, and distinct.
- Preserve player agency while showing that choices occur inside an unequal system.
- Punch upward at power and institutional behavior, not at workers trapped inside it.
- Use repetition with variation: the same corporate ritual becomes darker as the player understands its consequences.
- Keep jargon short enough to remain funny and readable during play.

---

## Prototype Scope

A focused prototype could cover one in-game workweek and include:

- One office room with six chickens and one rooster.
- A pecking minigame that produces eggs while generating fatigue.
- Five daily quotas with conflicting performance metrics.
- Basic relationships with two coworkers.
- One leaderboard and one wellness mechanic.
- Three major events: Mandatory Fun Friday, The Golden Egg, and Flock Restructuring.
- An end-of-week farmer presentation that reinterprets the player’s actions.
- Three outcomes: promotion, solidarity, or termination.

The prototype succeeds if players enjoy the pecking loop, laugh at the presentation, care about at least one coworker, and feel uneasy when the game rewards them for exploiting that coworker.

---

## One-Sentence Creative North Star

*Make the player laugh at a ridiculous chicken office, then realize they have been optimizing the very system the joke is about.*
